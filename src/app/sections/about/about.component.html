Turning ideas into products for over 25 years.

I discovered code twenty-five years ago and haven't really stopped since. The thrill of typing something out of nothing and watching it come alive is still the thing.

Most of what I make starts as a prototype — a way to teach myself something new or prove a concept I can't shake. Some of them turn into companies. Most don't, and that's the point: the practice keeps the muscles honed for whatever problem walks in next.

Flying is my biggest passion and the one I get the least time with. I'm always thinking about the next flight and when it does happen, it tends to be worth the wait: a breakfast run with the kids, or the whole family loaded up for a trip to the beach.

$ cat ~/principles.md
  1. Speed and quality aren't a tradeoff. After 25 years of practice, shipping fast is shipping well — prototypes leave my hands production-ready, not vibe-coded.
  2. Write for the next human. Code gets read far more than it gets written. If a teammate can't follow it on first pass, it isn't done.
  3. Pay the cost up front. A few extra days spent picking the right solution saves months of maintenance. Shortcuts charge compounding interest.
  4. Build with the user, not for an imagined one. Stay close to the people actually using the thing — their reality always beats the spec.
